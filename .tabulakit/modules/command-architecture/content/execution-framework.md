# Execution Framework

The bridge between planning and completion. An OPORD defines *what* to accomplish; the execution framework ensures clear handoff, provides mechanisms to adapt, and closes the learning loop.

## The Lifecycle

```
OPORD → Execution Brief → Execute → FRAGO (if needed) → AAR → Closure
```

## Phases

### 1. OPORD (Operations Order)

The plan is issued. See [Orders & Communication](orders-communication.md) for format.

**Key elements:**
- Situation (context and constraints)
- Mission (task and purpose)
- Execution (how, who does what)
- Sustainment (resources, logistics)
- Command & Signal (authority, communication)

### 2. Execution Brief (Back-Brief)

The person or team executing **briefs back** their understanding of the plan. This is not a formality — it's the critical quality check.

**The executor presents:**
- "Here's what I understand the mission to be..."
- "Here's how I plan to accomplish it..."
- "Here's what I need..."
- "Here are the risks I see..."

**The commander listens for:**
- Do they understand the intent (the *why*)?
- Is their approach reasonable?
- Have they identified the key risks?
- Can they adapt if conditions change?

**If the back-brief reveals misunderstanding, fix it now.** This is far cheaper than discovering it during execution.

### 3. Execute

Do the work. Track progress against the plan.

**During execution:**
- Report progress at agreed intervals
- Flag deviations from the plan early
- Make decisions at the lowest appropriate level
- When in doubt, refer to commander's intent

### 4. FRAGO (Fragmentary Order)

When conditions change, issue a FRAGO — a change order that modifies the original OPORD.

**When to FRAGO:**
- New information invalidates assumptions
- Timeline changes significantly
- Resources become unavailable
- A better approach is discovered

**FRAGO format:**
- What changed
- What's different now
- What stays the same
- Effective when

### 5. After Action Review (AAR)

Structured reflection on what happened and why.

**Four questions:**
1. What was planned?
2. What actually happened?
3. Why was there a difference?
4. What will we do differently?

**AAR rules:**
- No rank in the room — everyone speaks freely
- Focus on events and decisions, not people
- Capture specific, actionable lessons
- Assign owners to action items

### 6. Closure

Document lessons learned, update standing procedures, close out the operation.

- Were success criteria met?
- What permanent changes should be made?
- What knowledge needs to be preserved?

## Key Principle

> **Centralized planning, decentralized execution.** Plan together, then trust people to execute. If you've communicated intent clearly and confirmed understanding through the back-brief, the team can adapt to changing conditions without waiting for permission.
