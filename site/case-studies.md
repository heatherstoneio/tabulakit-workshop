# Case Studies

These are real examples of how this system is being used today. They're meant to help you think about what *you* might build at the workshop.

## Running a Business with the Command Architecture

**The problem:** A small technology company was trying to manage everything — product development, customer relationships, hiring, marketing, infrastructure, finances — and it was all tangled together in the founder's head, scattered across emails, and buried in documents nobody could find.

**What they built:** A central documentation site organized around seven areas of responsibility: personnel, intelligence, operations, logistics, planning, communications, and external messaging. Each area has its own section with the key questions that need answering, current status, and links to supporting documents.

**The result:** Instead of "everything is my problem all at once," the founder can now think in terms of "this is an operations question" or "this is a logistics question." The framework doesn't add bureaucracy — it provides a mental checklist that ensures nothing falls through the cracks. When a new team member joins, they can get up to speed by reading the site instead of relying on the founder to download everything from their brain.

**Workshop connection:** If you feel like you're juggling too many things and can't see the big picture, this approach might be for you. The Command Architecture module is available as an add-on during setup.

## Planning a Large-Scale Exercise

**The problem:** A military unit needed to plan and execute a multi-day field exercise involving dozens of personnel, multiple agencies, equipment logistics, and a compressed timeline. Information was spread across group texts, emails, phone calls, and a shared drive that nobody could navigate.

**What they built:** A mission planning site with sections for the timeline, personnel roster, communications log, planning checklists, risk register, and formal planning documents. The communications log became the single source of truth — every phone call, email, and decision got logged with a timestamp and linked to the relevant planning areas.

**The result:** When someone asked "what's the status of X?", the answer was on the site instead of in someone's memory. When plans changed (and they always do), the AI assistant helped update every affected section — change a date on the timeline, and it would flag which personnel and logistics entries also needed updating. The After Action Review was easy because the entire history of decisions and changes was already captured.

**Workshop connection:** If you're managing a complex project with lots of moving parts and people, the Mission template gives you this structure out of the box.

## Navigating a Complex Bureaucratic Process

**The problem:** A family was going through the U.S. immigration process — a years-long journey involving dozens of government forms, strict deadlines, supporting documents from multiple countries, attorney communications, and rules that change without warning. Missing one deadline or one document could set the entire process back months.

**What they built:** A project site tracking every step of the process: which forms were filed and when, what documents were gathered, what was still needed, deadlines, attorney notes, and a running log of every communication with every agency. Supporting documents were organized by category and linked from the relevant sections.

**The result:** What was previously a shoebox of anxiety became a clear picture of exactly where things stood. When the attorney asked for a document, they could find it in seconds instead of hours. When a rule changed, they could quickly assess what it affected. The communications log alone saved countless hours of "wait, what did they say about that?" conversations.

**Workshop connection:** If you're managing any kind of complex, multi-step process where missing a detail has real consequences — legal proceedings, regulatory compliance, construction permits, grant applications — this approach can bring order to chaos.

## Building a Course

**The problem:** An instructor was developing a hands-on workshop and needed a way to organize the syllabus, session materials, pre-work assignments, and resources — and make all of it accessible to students before, during, and after the course.

**What they built:** A course site with the syllabus, schedule, pre-work checklist, session outlines, and resource links. The pre-work page was the actual pre-work — students could access it to prepare. The workshop exercises were built right into the site, creating a recursive experience where students used the tool to learn about the tool.

**The result:** Students showed up prepared because the pre-work was clear and accessible. During the workshop, they could follow along at their own pace. After the workshop, the site remained as a reference they could return to. And the instructor could update materials at any time — changes went live automatically.

**Workshop connection:** This is literally the site you're reading right now. The Course template gives you this structure, and the workshop itself is the proof that it works.

## What These Have in Common

Every one of these examples started with the same situation: important information scattered across too many places, and a person spending too much mental energy just trying to keep track of it all.

The technology isn't the point. The point is **getting organized so you can focus on what actually matters** — running your business, completing your project, teaching your students, or just reducing the background anxiety of feeling like you're forgetting something important.

At the workshop, you'll build something like one of these examples. Come with a problem in mind, and you'll leave with a solution that's already working.
